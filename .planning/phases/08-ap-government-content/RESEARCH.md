## Required SCOTUS Cases (from PDF, pp. 28–32)

All 14 required SCOTUS cases:

1. **Marbury v. Madison (1803)** — Established judicial review; Supreme Court can declare acts of Congress/executive unconstitutional. Unit 1, 2.
2. **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)** — Established federal supremacy over states; upheld implied powers/Necessary and Proper Clause. Units 1, 2.
3. **Schenck v. United States (1919)** — Speech creating "clear and present danger" not protected by 1st Amendment. Units 1, 3.
4. **Brown v. Board of Education (1954)** — Race-based school segregation violates Equal Protection Clause (14th Amendment). Units 2, 3.
5. **Baker v. Carr (1962)** — Redistricting justiciable in federal courts under Equal Protection Clause. Units 1, 2, 5.
6. **Engel v. Vitale (1962)** — School-sponsored prayer violates Establishment Clause. Units 1, 3.
7. **Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)** — 6th Amendment right to counsel applies to state felony defendants (selective incorporation). Units 1, 3.
8. **Tinker v. Des Moines (1969)** — Students' political symbolic speech (armbands) protected by 1st Amendment in public schools. Units 1, 3.
9. **New York Times Co. v. United States (1971)** — "Heavy presumption against prior restraint" of press; bolstered 1st Amendment press freedoms. Units 1, 2, 3.
10. **Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972)** — Compelling Amish students past 8th grade violates Free Exercise Clause. Units 1, 3.
11. **Shaw v. Reno (1993)** — Race cannot be the sole factor in drawing majority-minority congressional districts (14th Amendment). Units 1, 2, 5.
12. **United States v. Lopez (1995)** — Congress exceeded Commerce Clause power by making gun possession in school zones a federal crime. Units 1, 2.
13. **McDonald v. Chicago (2010)** — 2nd Amendment right to bear arms for self-defense is incorporated to states. Units 1, 3.
14. **Citizens United v. FEC (2010)** — Corporate/union political spending is protected speech under 1st Amendment. Units 1, 2, 5.

**Unit assignments for this phase:**
- Unit 1 cases: Marbury v. Madison, McCulloch v. Maryland, United States v. Lopez (federalism-related)
- Unit 2 cases: Marbury v. Madison (judicial review), Baker v. Carr (redistricting/gerrymandering), Shaw v. Reno (redistricting), Citizens United (campaign finance)
- Unit 4 cases: None required directly, but Citizens United relates to political participation/party ideology context

## Per-Unit Research

### Unit 1: Foundations of American Democracy (15–22%, ~9 topics)

**Topics:** 1.1 Ideals of Democracy | 1.2 Types of Democracy | 1.3 Government Power and Individual Rights | 1.4 Challenges of the Articles of Confederation | 1.5 Ratification of the U.S. Constitution | 1.6 Principles of American Government | 1.7 Relationship Between States and National Government | 1.8 Constitutional Interpretations of Federalism | 1.9 Federalism in Action

**Key concepts:**
- Natural rights, social contract, popular sovereignty, limited government
- Separation of powers, checks and balances, federalism, republicanism
- Three models of representative democracy: participatory, pluralist, elite
- Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists: Federalist No. 10 (large republic controls factions), Brutus No. 1 (dangers of large central government)
- Articles of Confederation weaknesses: no executive, no national courts, no taxing power, no regulation of interstate commerce, Shays' Rebellion
- Constitutional Convention compromises: Great Compromise (bicameral Congress), Electoral College, Three-Fifths Compromise, 1808 slave trade postponement, promise of Bill of Rights
- Amendment process: 2/3 of both houses + 3/4 states ratification
- Federalism power types: enumerated/expressed, implied (Necessary and Proper), reserved (10th Amendment), concurrent
- Fiscal federalism: categorical grants (most common, most restrictive), block grants (broad, state-preferred), revenue sharing (least used, no restrictions), mandates
- SCOTUS: McCulloch v. Maryland (implied powers), United States v. Lopez (Commerce Clause limits)

**Foundational Documents in Unit 1:**
- Articles of Confederation (weaknesses)
- Brutus No. 1 (Anti-Federalist warning about large republic)
- Declaration of Independence (natural rights, popular sovereignty)
- Federalist No. 10 (controlling factions in large republic)
- Federalist No. 51 (separation of powers/checks and balances rationale)
- Federalist No. 70 (justification for strong single executive)

**Key figures:** Thomas Jefferson (Declaration), James Madison (Constitution, Federalist 10, 51), Alexander Hamilton (Federalist 70, 78), George Washington (Constitutional Convention), Publius (pen name for Federalist authors)

**is_key_term candidates:** Social Contract, Popular Sovereignty, Federalism, Checks and Balances, Separation of Powers, Necessary and Proper Clause, Supremacy Clause, Reserved Powers, Concurrent Powers, Categorical Grants, Marbury v. Madison, McCulloch v. Maryland

### Unit 2: Interactions Among Branches of Government (25–36%, ~15 topics)

**Topics:** 2.1 Congress: Senate and House | 2.2 Structures, Powers, Functions of Congress | 2.3 Congressional Behavior | 2.4 Roles and Powers of the President | 2.5 Checks on the Presidency | 2.6 Expansion of Presidential Power | 2.7 Presidential Communication | 2.8 The Judicial Branch | 2.9 Role of the Judicial Branch | 2.10 The Court in Action | 2.11 Checks on the Judicial Branch | 2.12 The Bureaucracy | 2.13 Discretionary and Rulemaking Authority | 2.14 Holding the Bureaucracy Accountable | 2.15 Policy and the Branches of Government

**Congress:**
- Senate: 100 members, 6-year terms, 1/3 elected every 2 years (continuous body), filibuster, cloture (60 votes), unanimous consent, holds, president pro tempore, Senate Majority Leader
- House: 435 members, 2-year terms, Speaker of the House, Rules Committee (sets debate rules), discharge petition, revenue bills originate here, Committee of the Whole
- Both: bicameral, committees (mark up bills, determine leadership by majority party), conference committees, enumerated + implied powers
- Congressional powers: pass budget, raise taxes, borrow money, coin money, declare war, fund military, naturalization, regulate interstate commerce, create federal courts, oversight via Necessary and Proper Clause
- Budget: mandatory spending (entitlements — Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid), discretionary spending (defense, education, infrastructure)
- Pork-barrel legislation, logrolling
- Congressional behavior: partisan voting, polarization, gridlock, divided government, lame duck
- Representative roles: trustee (votes own judgment), delegate (votes constituent wishes), politico (combination)
- Gerrymandering, redistricting — Baker v. Carr, Shaw v. Reno

**President:**
- Formal powers: veto (2/3 override), pocket veto (cannot be overridden), commander-in-chief, treaties (2/3 Senate ratification), Senate confirmation of appointments
- Informal powers: executive agreements (no Senate approval), executive orders, signing statements, bargaining and persuasion, bully pulpit, State of the Union
- Cabinet, Vice President, Executive Office of the President (EOP)
- 22nd Amendment (term limits — concern about expansion of power)
- Federalist No. 70 (justification for single strong executive)

**Judiciary:**
- Article III establishes judicial branch; Federalist No. 78 argues for judicial independence
- Marbury v. Madison established judicial review
- Stare decisis (legal precedent), rule of four (cert granted if 4 justices agree)
- Life tenure — independence from political pressure, can issue unpopular decisions
- Judicial activism vs. judicial restraint
- Majority opinion, concurring opinion, dissenting opinion
- Congressional checks on courts: legislation, constitutional amendments, appointments, jurisdiction-stripping

**Bureaucracy:**
- Departments, agencies, commissions, government corporations
- Functions: write/enforce regulations, issue fines, testify before Congress, implement policy
- Iron triangles (congressional committee + bureaucratic agency + interest group)
- Issue networks (temporary coalitions)
- Civil service/merit system vs. political patronage/spoils system
- Delegated discretionary authority — rulemaking authority
- Key agencies: DHS, DOT, DVA, Dept of Education, EPA, FEC, SEC
- Congressional oversight: review/monitor, investigate/hearings, power of the purse
- Presidential control: ideology, compliance monitoring

**Foundational Documents in Unit 2:** Federalist No. 51, Federalist No. 70, Federalist No. 78

**Key figures:** James Madison (Federalist 51, 10), Alexander Hamilton (Federalist 70, 78), Chief Justice John Marshall (Marbury v. Madison, McCulloch)

**is_key_term candidates:** Filibuster, Cloture, Gerrymandering, Trustee Model, Delegate Model, Judicial Review, Stare Decisis, Executive Order, Iron Triangle, Congressional Oversight, Marbury v. Madison, Baker v. Carr, Shaw v. Reno, Citizens United v. FEC

### Unit 4: American Political Ideologies and Beliefs (10–15%, ~10 topics)

**Topics:** 4.1 American Attitudes About Government and Politics | 4.2 Political Socialization | 4.3 Changes in Ideology | 4.4 Influence of Political Events on Ideology | 4.5 Measuring Public Opinion | 4.6 Evaluating Public Opinion Data | 4.7 Ideologies of Political Parties | 4.8 Ideology and Policymaking | 4.9 Ideology and Economic Policy | 4.10 Ideology and Social Policy

**Key concepts:**
- Core American values: individualism, equality of opportunity, free enterprise, rule of law
- Political socialization: family, schools, peers, media, civic/religious organizations
- Generational effects vs. life cycle effects on ideology
- Political ideology: liberal (more government intervention), conservative (less government/federalism), libertarian (minimal government)
- Public opinion polling types: opinion polls, benchmark polls, tracking polls, exit polls
- Polling methodology: accurate sampling, margin of error, neutral question wording, accurate reporting
- Party ideologies: Democrats (liberal), Republicans (conservative)
- Fiscal policy (Congress + President): Keynesian vs. supply-side
- Monetary policy (Federal Reserve): interest rates, max employment + price stability
- Social policy: liberal (national gov't involvement), conservative (state authority), libertarian (minimal involvement)
- Valence issues (broad consensus) vs. wedge issues (divisive)
- Linkage institutions: political parties, interest groups, elections, media

**is_key_term candidates:** Political Socialization, Political Ideology, Liberalism, Conservatism, Libertarianism, Fiscal Policy, Monetary Policy, Public Opinion Poll, Tracking Poll, Exit Poll, Margin of Error, Valence Issue, Wedge Issue
