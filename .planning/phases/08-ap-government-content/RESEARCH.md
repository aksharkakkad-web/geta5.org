## Summary

AP US Government and Politics is a 5-unit course covering the foundations, institutions, rights, ideologies, and political participation of the American political system. The exam consists of 55 MCQs in 80 minutes plus 4 FRQs in 100 minutes. The exam date for 2026 is May 5.

The course has 9 Required Foundational Documents and 15 Required Supreme Court Cases (updated in 2023-24 to include Griswold v. Connecticut and Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization). These 24 required items are central to the exam and must be thoroughly covered in drills, MCQs, and study guides.

## 9 Required Foundational Documents

1. **Declaration of Independence (1776)** - Thomas Jefferson - Natural rights, social contract, right to revolution
2. **Articles of Confederation (1781)** - Continental Congress - Weak national government, strong state authority
3. **Constitution of the United States (1787)** - Constitutional Convention - Federal framework, separation of powers, checks and balances
4. **Federalist No. 10 (1787)** - James Madison - Factions inevitable but controlled through large republic
5. **Brutus No. 1 (1787)** - Anti-Federalist (Robert Yates attributed) - Large republics threaten liberty through concentrated power
6. **Federalist No. 51 (1788)** - James Madison - Separation of powers and checks prevent tyranny; "ambition must counteract ambition"
7. **Federalist No. 70 (1788)** - Alexander Hamilton - Strong single executive ensures energy, accountability, effectiveness
8. **Federalist No. 78 (1788)** - Alexander Hamilton - Independent judiciary with life tenure, judicial review, "least dangerous branch"
9. **Letter from Birmingham Jail (1963)** - Martin Luther King Jr. - Moral duty of nonviolent resistance against unjust laws

## 15 Required Supreme Court Cases

1. **Marbury v. Madison (1803)** - Established judicial review; Supreme Court can declare laws unconstitutional
2. **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)** - Implied powers via Necessary and Proper Clause; states cannot tax federal institutions; supremacy of federal law
3. **Schenck v. United States (1919)** - Established "clear and present danger" test for limiting free speech; government can restrict speech that poses immediate danger
4. **Brown v. Board of Education (1954)** - Overturned "separate but equal" (Plessy); segregated schools violate Equal Protection Clause of 14th Amendment
5. **Baker v. Carr (1962)** - Redistricting is justiciable (courts can rule on it); led to "one person, one vote" principle
6. **Engel v. Vitale (1962)** - School-sponsored prayer violates Establishment Clause; government cannot compose or mandate prayers in public schools
7. **Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)** - Right to an attorney in felony cases under 6th Amendment; incorporated to states via 14th Amendment
8. **Tinker v. Des Moines (1969)** - Students retain free speech rights in schools; symbolic speech protected unless substantially disruptive
9. **New York Times Co. v. United States (1971)** - Prior restraint on press publication presumptively unconstitutional; Pentagon Papers case
10. **Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972)** - Free Exercise Clause protects Amish families' right to withdraw children from school after 8th grade
11. **Roe v. Wade (1973)** - Right to privacy includes abortion access (now overturned by Dobbs but still part of CED Topic 3.9)
12. **Shaw v. Reno (1993)** - Race cannot be predominant factor in drawing district lines; racial gerrymandering violates Equal Protection
13. **United States v. Lopez (1995)** - Limited Commerce Clause power; federal Gun-Free School Zones Act exceeded congressional authority
14. **McDonald v. Chicago (2010)** - 2nd Amendment right to bear arms incorporated to states via 14th Amendment Due Process Clause
15. **Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission (2010)** - Political spending is protected speech; corporations/unions can make unlimited independent expenditures

**2023-24 Update:** Griswold v. Connecticut (1965) and Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022) added to Topic 3.9 content alongside Roe v. Wade. None of the three are used for FRQ 3 (SCOTUS comparison), but all three are testable in MCQ context.

## Key Constitutional Amendments for Coverage

- **1st Amendment** - Religion, speech, press, assembly, petition
- **2nd Amendment** - Right to bear arms
- **4th Amendment** - Search and seizure protections
- **5th Amendment** - Due process, double jeopardy, self-incrimination, eminent domain
- **6th Amendment** - Right to counsel, speedy trial, jury trial
- **8th Amendment** - Cruel and unusual punishment, excessive bail
- **10th Amendment** - Reserved powers to states
- **13th Amendment** - Abolition of slavery
- **14th Amendment** - Due process, equal protection, citizenship, incorporation
- **15th Amendment** - Voting rights regardless of race
- **19th Amendment** - Women's suffrage
- **24th Amendment** - Abolition of poll taxes
- **26th Amendment** - Voting age lowered to 18
