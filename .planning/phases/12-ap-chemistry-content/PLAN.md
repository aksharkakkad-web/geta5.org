## Unit 1: Atomic Structure and Properties

### is_key_term Candidates (9)
mole, molar mass, isotope, empirical formula, electron configuration, effective nuclear charge, ionization energy, electronegativity, Avogadro's number

### Drill Plan (35 cards)

**definition_to_term (14 cards):**
1. The SI unit representing $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ particles of a substance → `mole`
2. The mass in grams of one mole of a substance → `molar mass`
3. Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons → `isotopes`
4. The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound → `empirical formula`
5. A representation of how electrons are distributed among orbitals in an atom → `electron configuration`
6. The net positive charge experienced by a valence electron after accounting for shielding → `effective nuclear charge`
7. The energy required to remove the outermost electron from a gaseous atom → `ionization energy`
8. An atom's ability to attract electrons toward itself in a chemical bond → `electronegativity`
9. Electrons in the outermost shell of an atom that participate in bonding → `valence electrons`
10. Electrons in inner shells that shield valence electrons from nuclear charge → `core electrons`
11. The principle that electrons occupy the lowest available energy subshell before higher ones → `Aufbau principle`
12. The process of shielding valence electrons from the full nuclear charge by inner electrons → `electron shielding`
13. The technique that measures ionization energies of electrons at different energy levels, used to determine electron configuration → `photoelectron spectroscopy`
14. The graph of binding energy vs. relative number of electrons produced by photoelectron spectroscopy → `PES spectrum`

**significance_to_person (7 cards):**
1. Determined that atoms contain a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus via gold foil experiment → `Rutherford`
2. Proposed quantized energy levels for electrons orbiting the nucleus → `Bohr`
3. Described the law of electrostatic attraction and repulsion between charges → `Coulomb`
4. His number ($6.022 \times 10^{23}$) defines the mole → `Avogadro`
5. Arranged elements by increasing atomic number revealing periodic trends → `Mendeleev`
6. Proposed discrete energy packets (quanta) for light emission → `Planck`
7. Demonstrated that light has both wave and particle properties in the photoelectric effect → `Einstein`

**concept_mc (14 cards):**
1. Which element has the highest first ionization energy among Period 2 elements?
2. Why does atomic radius decrease across Period 2 from left to right?
3. A mass spectrum shows two peaks at m/z = 10 and m/z = 11. What does this indicate?
4. An element has 35% of isotope A (mass 10) and 65% of isotope B (mass 11). What is the average atomic mass?
5. Which of the following electron configurations represents a ground-state atom with the highest effective nuclear charge?
6. A PES spectrum shows three peaks. What does each peak represent?
7. Why does fluorine have a higher ionization energy than oxygen despite being in the same period?
8. Which of the following best explains the increase in ionization energy from Li to Ne?
9. An element forms a 2+ ion. Which row in the table is consistent with this element?
10. Which property would best identify an unknown element as a metal rather than a nonmetal?
11. How does adding a neutron to an atom change its atomic number, mass number, and chemical behavior?
12. Why does the second ionization energy of sodium increase dramatically compared to the first?
13. Which factor most accounts for the trend in electronegativity across Period 3?
14. A student measures the empirical formula of a compound. What additional information is needed to determine the molecular formula?

### MCQ Plan (55 questions)
- **Easy (11):** Mole calculations, isotope definition, periodic trend direction, electron configuration notation
- **Medium (25):** Average atomic mass from mass spectrum, PES interpretation, periodic trend explanations using Coulomb's law, ionic charge from electron configuration
- **Hard (19):** Multi-step mole calculations, complex PES analysis, comparing trends across multiple groups/periods, predicting properties from electron configuration

**Stimulus targets:** ~38 of 55 questions have stimulus
- Tables: mass spectrum data, isotope abundance tables, periodic table data
- Charts: PES spectra, ionization energy graphs

### Drill Plan (35 cards)

4. The phenomenon where a molecule cannot be represented by a single Lewis structure → `resonance`
5. The theory that predicts molecular geometry based on electron pair repulsions → `VSEPR`
6. The orbital mixing that explains molecular geometry; sp, sp², sp³ are examples → `hybridization`
7. A bond formed by end-to-end orbital overlap → `sigma bond`
8. A bond formed by side-to-side orbital overlap between p orbitals → `pi bond`
9. The number of bonding pairs between two atoms in a covalent bond → `bond order`
10. The average energy required to break one mole of a specific bond in the gas phase → `bond energy`
11. The average distance between two bonded atoms' nuclei → `bond length`
12. The model that describes metallic bonding as positive ions surrounded by a mobile "sea" of electrons → `sea of electrons model`
13. The bond type between atoms of very different electronegativity where electrons transfer completely → `ionic bond`
14. The property describing the arrangement of atoms in space around a central atom → `molecular geometry`
15. The bond type between atoms with very small electronegativity difference → `nonpolar covalent bond`
16. The hybridization present in a molecule with all single bonds and no lone pairs on the central atom → `sp³ hybridization`

## Unit 3: Properties of Substances and Mixtures

### is_key_term Candidates (12)
London dispersion forces, hydrogen bonding, ideal gas law, kinetic molecular theory, molarity, Beer-Lambert law, Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, dipole-dipole forces, ionic solid, covalent network solid, chromatography, molar absorptivity

### Drill Plan (40 cards)

**definition_to_term (18 cards):**
1. Temporary attractive forces caused by instantaneous dipoles; present in all molecules → `London dispersion forces`
2. The strongest type of intermolecular force; occurs when H is bonded to N, O, or F → `hydrogen bonding`
3. Attractive forces between permanent dipoles on adjacent polar molecules → `dipole-dipole forces`
4. The law $PV = nRT$ describing the behavior of an ideal gas → `ideal gas law`
5. A model describing gas particles as point masses in constant random motion with elastic collisions → `kinetic molecular theory`
6. The distribution showing the range of molecular speeds at a given temperature → `Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution`
7. The number of moles of solute per liter of solution → `molarity`
8. The law $A = \varepsilon bc$ relating absorbance to concentration, path length, and molar absorptivity → `Beer-Lambert law`
9. The proportionality constant in Beer-Lambert law that characterizes how strongly a substance absorbs light at a given wavelength → `molar absorptivity`
10. A solid held together by ionic bonds in a regular crystal lattice → `ionic solid`
11. A solid in which atoms are covalently bonded throughout the entire structure → `covalent network solid`
12. A solid held together only by intermolecular forces between discrete molecules → `molecular solid`
13. A solid characterized by delocalized electrons and metallic bonding throughout → `metallic solid`
14. The separation technique that uses differences in intermolecular forces to separate components → `chromatography`
15. The principle that temperature of a gas is directly proportional to the average kinetic energy of its particles → `kinetic energy–temperature relationship`
16. The condition at which gas particles would have no volume and no intermolecular forces — the ideal limit → `ideal gas conditions`
17. Attractive forces between ions and polar solvent molecules; key in dissolution → `ion-dipole forces`
18. The energy absorbed or released when a substance changes phase at constant temperature → `enthalpy of phase change`

**significance_to_person (6 cards):**
1. Developed the law relating gas pressure, volume, and temperature to moles → `Gay-Lussac / Combined Gas Law`
2. Used the equation $A = \varepsilon bc$ to quantify how substances absorb light → `Beer-Lambert`
3. Proposed that gas molecules have a distribution of speeds that depends on temperature → `Maxwell-Boltzmann`
4. Published the ideal gas equation combining earlier gas laws into $PV = nRT$ → `Clapeyron / Ideal Gas Law`
5. Developed kinetic molecular theory explaining gas behavior at the molecular level → `KMT`
6. Observed that gases at low pressure exert less attractive force than real gases — motivation for van der Waals corrections → `van der Waals`

## Unit 5: Kinetics

### is_key_term Candidates (11)
reaction rate, rate law, rate constant, reaction order, half-life, activation energy, transition state, reaction intermediate, rate-limiting step, catalyst, collision theory

### Drill Plan (35 cards)

**definition_to_term (16 cards):**
1. The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time → `reaction rate`
2. The mathematical expression $\text{rate} = k[A]^m[B]^n$ relating rate to concentrations → `rate law`
3. The proportionality constant in the rate law, dependent only on temperature → `rate constant`
4. The sum of the exponents in the rate law expression → `reaction order`
5. The time required for the concentration of a reactant to decrease by half → `half-life`
6. The minimum energy colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur → `activation energy`
7. The highest-energy species along the reaction coordinate; not isolable → `transition state`
8. A species produced in one elementary step and consumed in a later step → `reaction intermediate`
9. The slowest elementary step in a mechanism that determines the overall rate → `rate-limiting step`
10. A substance that lowers activation energy and is regenerated after the reaction → `catalyst`
11. The model explaining reactions as requiring sufficient energy AND proper orientation upon collision → `collision theory`
12. The integrated rate law where a graph of $\ln[A]$ vs. time is linear → `first-order reaction`
13. The integrated rate law where a graph of $1/[A]$ vs. time is linear → `second-order reaction`
14. The integrated rate law where a graph of $[A]$ vs. time is linear → `zeroth-order reaction`
15. The sequence of elementary steps that describes how a reaction proceeds at the molecular level → `reaction mechanism`
16. A catalyst that is in the same phase as the reactants → `homogeneous catalyst`

**significance_to_person (3 cards):**
1. Proposed the collision theory model explaining the molecular basis of reaction rates → `Arrhenius`
2. Developed the Arrhenius equation relating rate constant to activation energy and temperature → `Arrhenius`
3. Proposed the transition state theory (activated complex theory) to explain activation energy → `Eyring`

5. How does a catalyst affect the enthalpy change of a reaction?

7. A reaction energy profile shows two humps. What does the intermediate correspond to?
8. Which statement best explains why increasing temperature increases reaction rate?
9. Why does increasing surface area of a solid reactant increase the reaction rate?
10. A student determines the rate law experimentally. If doubling [A] quadruples the rate, what is the order with respect to A?
11. Which graph would confirm a second-order reaction?

- **Medium (25):** Rate law determination from data, half-life calculations, energy diagram interpretation

## Unit 6: Thermochemistry

### is_key_term Candidates (10)
endothermic, exothermic, enthalpy, calorimetry, specific heat capacity, heat of fusion, heat of vaporization, Hess's Law, standard enthalpy of formation, bond enthalpy

### Drill Plan (35 cards)

**definition_to_term (17 cards):**
1. A process in which the system absorbs heat from the surroundings ($\Delta H > 0$) → `endothermic`
2. A process in which the system releases heat to the surroundings ($\Delta H < 0$) → `exothermic`
3. The heat content of a system at constant pressure; symbol $H$ → `enthalpy`
4. The experimental technique of measuring heat transfer using an insulated container → `calorimetry`
5. The energy required to raise 1 gram of a substance by 1°C; symbol $c$ → `specific heat capacity`
6. The enthalpy change for melting 1 mole of a solid at its melting point → `molar enthalpy of fusion`
7. The enthalpy change for vaporizing 1 mole of liquid at its boiling point → `molar enthalpy of vaporization`
8. The principle that the total enthalpy change of a reaction is the sum of individual steps → `Hess's Law`
9. The enthalpy change when 1 mole of a compound forms from its elements in their standard states → `standard enthalpy of formation`
10. The energy required to break one mole of a specific covalent bond in the gas phase → `bond enthalpy`
11. The law stating that the internal energy of the universe is constant → `first law of thermodynamics`
12. The mathematical relationship $q = mc\Delta T$ for calculating heat transfer → `calorimetry equation`
13. A calorimeter with constant pressure, used for solution-phase reactions → `coffee-cup calorimeter`
14. A sealed calorimeter measuring heat at constant volume → `bomb calorimeter`
15. The temperature at which solid and liquid phases coexist — transition is isothermal → `melting point`
16. Standard conditions used in thermochemistry: 25°C (298 K) and 1 atm → `standard conditions`
17. The sign convention where negative $\Delta H$ indicates heat released to surroundings → `exothermic sign convention`

**significance_to_person (3 cards):**
1. Developed the law that total enthalpy change is path-independent — the principle behind Hess's Law → `Hess`
2. Formulated conservation of energy as the first law of thermodynamics → `Joule`
3. Established the measurement of heat as a form of energy and standardized the calorimetry approach → `Lavoisier`

### MCQ Plan (55 questions)

- **Medium (25):** Calorimetry calculations, Hess's Law application, bond enthalpy calculations

## Unit 7: Equilibrium

### is_key_term Candidates (10)
equilibrium constant, reaction quotient, Le Châtelier's principle, ICE table, solubility product constant, common-ion effect, dynamic equilibrium, $K_c$, $K_p$, $K_{sp}$

### Drill Plan (35 cards)

**definition_to_term (18 cards):**
1. The state where forward and reverse reaction rates are equal and concentrations remain constant → `dynamic equilibrium`
2. The ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, each raised to stoichiometric exponents → `equilibrium constant`
3. The equilibrium constant expression written in terms of molar concentrations → `$K_c$`
4. The equilibrium constant expression written in terms of partial pressures → `$K_p$`
5. A ratio of product to reactant concentrations at any point in a reaction (not necessarily at equilibrium) → `reaction quotient`
6. A tool for tracking concentration changes from initial to equilibrium: Initial, Change, Equilibrium → `ICE table`
7. The principle that a system at equilibrium will shift to oppose a stress applied to it → `Le Châtelier's principle`
8. The equilibrium constant for the dissolution of a sparingly soluble salt → `solubility product constant`
9. The reduction in solubility of a slightly soluble salt when a common ion is added → `common-ion effect`
10. The shift in equilibrium that occurs when the concentration of a product or reactant is changed → `concentration stress`
11. A large value of $K$ (much greater than 1) indicates this position of equilibrium → `products favored`
12. A small value of $K$ (much less than 1) indicates this position of equilibrium → `reactants favored`
13. If $Q < K$, the reaction proceeds in this direction to reach equilibrium → `forward direction`
14. If $Q > K$, the reaction proceeds in this direction to reach equilibrium → `reverse direction`
15. Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from the equilibrium expression because this quantity remains essentially constant → `concentration of pure solids/liquids`
16. When $K$ for a reaction is $1/K$ for the reverse, the relationship is called this → `inverse relationship`
17. The temperature at which a reaction's equilibrium constant $K$ = 1 → `crossover temperature`
18. When two reactions with constants $K_1$ and $K_2$ are added, the overall constant equals → `$K_1 \times K_2$`

**significance_to_person (4 cards):**
1. Proposed the principle that equilibrium systems respond to minimize applied stresses → `Le Châtelier`
2. Developed the equilibrium law and mass action expression → `Guldberg and Waage`
3. Extended equilibrium concepts to acid-base systems using Ka and Kb → `Brønsted`
4. Applied equilibrium principles to solubility in developing $K_{sp}$ → `Nernst`

**concept_mc (13 cards):**

6. $Q > K$ for a reaction. Which direction will the reaction proceed?
7. A student uses an ICE table to find equilibrium concentrations. What information is needed?
8. How does adding $NaCl(aq)$ affect the solubility of $AgCl(s)$?
9. For a reaction, $K_c = 100$. If all concentrations are halved, what is the new value of $Q$?
10. Why are pure solids excluded from the equilibrium constant expression?
11. What happens to the equilibrium position when an inert gas is added to a closed system at constant volume?
12. A student finds $K_{sp}$ of $BaSO_4$ is $1.1 \times 10^{-10}$. What is the molar solubility?
13. Which stress would change the value of $K$?

### MCQ Plan (55 questions)
- **Easy (11):** Writing K expressions, Q vs K comparison, Le Châtelier qualitative
- **Medium (25):** ICE table calculations, Ksp and molar solubility, effect of stresses on equilibrium
- **Hard (19):** Complex ICE with approximations, common-ion effect calculations, combined equilibria

## Unit 8: Acids and Bases

### is_key_term Candidates (13)
pH, pOH, $K_w$, strong acid, weak acid, $K_a$, $K_b$, pKa, buffer, Henderson-Hasselbalch equation, equivalence point, half-equivalence point, buffer capacity

### Drill Plan (40 cards)

**definition_to_term (20 cards):**
1. A measure of hydrogen ion concentration: $\text{pH} = -\log[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+]$ → `pH`
2. A measure of hydroxide ion concentration: $\text{pOH} = -\log[\text{OH}^-]$ → `pOH`
3. The water dissociation constant: $K_w = [\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{OH}^-] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$ at 25°C → `$K_w$`
4. An acid that dissociates completely in water → `strong acid`
5. An acid that only partially dissociates in water → `weak acid`
6. The equilibrium constant for the ionization of a weak acid → `$K_a$`
7. The equilibrium constant for the ionization of a weak base → `$K_b$`
8. The negative logarithm of the acid dissociation constant: $\text{pK}_a = -\log K_a$ → `pKa`
9. A solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added → `buffer`
10. The equation $\text{pH} = \text{pK}_a + \log\frac{[\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}$ used to calculate buffer pH → `Henderson-Hasselbalch equation`
11. The point in a titration where moles of acid equal moles of base → `equivalence point`
12. The point in a titration halfway to the equivalence point where pH = pKa → `half-equivalence point`
13. The ability of a buffer to resist pH changes; increases with concentration → `buffer capacity`
14. A substance that changes color at a specific pH — used to detect equivalence point → `acid-base indicator`
15. The fraction of a weak acid that has ionized at equilibrium → `percent ionization`
16. An acid-base pair that differs by one proton → `conjugate acid-base pair`
17. A base that accepts only one proton → `monoprotic base`
18. An acid that can donate more than one proton — treated with multiple Ka values → `polyprotic acid`
19. A solution with pH below 7 at 25°C containing more $\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$ than $\text{OH}^-$ → `acidic solution`
20. A solution containing equal concentrations of conjugate acid and conjugate base → `buffer solution`

**significance_to_person (4 cards):**
1. Proposed the acid-base definition based on proton transfer → `Brønsted and Lowry`
2. Derived the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation for calculating buffer pH → `Henderson-Hasselbalch`
3. Established the pH scale and the concept of hydrogen ion concentration → `Sørensen`
4. Defined strong and weak electrolytes and the concept of degree of dissociation → `Arrhenius`

**concept_mc (16 cards):**
1. A 0.1 M HCl solution is prepared. What is the pH?
2. What is the pH at the half-equivalence point of a weak acid–strong base titration?
3. A student adds NaOH to a buffer. Which component of the buffer reacts?
4. Two weak acids have $K_a = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$ and $K_a = 6.3 \times 10^{-5}$. Which is stronger?
5. A titration curve shows a gradual slope before the equivalence point. What does this indicate?
6. Why does a buffer resist pH change when a small amount of acid is added?
7. The pKa of acetic acid is 4.74. At what pH does acetic acid best buffer?
8. A solution of NH₃ is prepared. Which ion is in excess: $\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$ or $\text{OH}^-$?
9. What is the pH at the equivalence point of a strong acid–strong base titration?
10. A buffer is prepared from equal moles of HA and A⁻. How does the pH compare to pKa?
11. Which pair would make the best buffer?
12. Why do strong acids have a higher percent ionization than weak acids at the same concentration?
13. A student measures pH = 9.2 for a solution. Is the solution acidic, basic, or neutral?
14. At the equivalence point of a weak acid–strong base titration, why is pH > 7?
15. Henderson-Hasselbalch requires equal concentrations of HA and A⁻ for what relationship between pH and pKa?
16. Which structural feature makes HCl a stronger acid than HF despite smaller electronegativity difference?

### MCQ Plan (65 questions)
- **Easy (13):** pH calculation for strong acids, identifying strong vs. weak acids, buffer definition
- **Medium (29):** Ka/Kb calculations, percent ionization, Henderson-Hasselbalch application, titration curve interpretation
- **Hard (23):** Buffer capacity analysis, polyprotic acid qualitative reasoning, complex Ka/Kb from Kw, pH-solubility relationships

## Unit 9: Thermodynamics and Electrochemistry

### is_key_term Candidates (12)
entropy, Gibbs free energy, thermodynamically favored, galvanic cell, electrolytic cell, anode, cathode, standard reduction potential, Faraday's constant, electrolysis, coupled reactions, Nernst equation

### Drill Plan (35 cards)

**definition_to_term (16 cards):**
1. A thermodynamic state function measuring the disorder or dispersal of energy in a system → `entropy`
2. The thermodynamic function $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$ that predicts whether a reaction is spontaneous → `Gibbs free energy`
3. A reaction in which $\Delta G < 0$ — thermodynamically proceeds without external energy input → `thermodynamically favored reaction`
4. An electrochemical cell that converts chemical energy to electrical energy (spontaneous redox) → `galvanic cell`
5. An electrochemical cell that uses electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous redox reaction → `electrolytic cell`
6. The electrode where oxidation occurs in any electrochemical cell → `anode`
7. The electrode where reduction occurs in any electrochemical cell → `cathode`
8. The voltage produced by a half-reaction under standard conditions relative to the standard hydrogen electrode → `standard reduction potential`
9. The constant $F = 96,485\ \text{C·mol}^{-1}\text{e}^-$ used in electrochemical calculations → `Faraday's constant`
10. The process of using electrical current to drive a non-spontaneous chemical reaction → `electrolysis`
11. Linking an unfavorable reaction to a favorable one so that the combined $\Delta G^\circ < 0$ → `coupled reactions`
12. The equation $E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - \frac{RT}{nF}\ln Q$ relating cell potential to Q → `Nernst equation`
13. Particles moving from regions of high concentration to low concentration → `diffusion`
14. Increased product concentrations indicate higher entropy; more complex molecules may have higher entropy due to this → `positional entropy`
15. When $\Delta G^\circ > 0$, the equilibrium constant is less than 1, meaning this position is favored → `reactants favored`
16. The ionic conductor that allows ion flow between half-cells without mixing solutions → `salt bridge`

**significance_to_person (5 cards):**
1. Introduced the concept of free energy and derived the criterion for thermodynamic favorability → `Gibbs`
2. Established the laws of electrolysis relating charge to moles of product → `Faraday`
3. Developed the equation relating cell potential to non-standard conditions → `Nernst`
4. Proposed the second law of thermodynamics introducing entropy → `Clausius`
5. Formulated the third law of thermodynamics setting absolute zero entropy for perfect crystals → `Nernst`

**concept_mc (14 cards):**
1. A reaction has $\Delta H < 0$ and $\Delta S < 0$. Under what conditions is $\Delta G < 0$?
2. Which process results in an increase in entropy?
3. A galvanic cell has $E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = +0.76\ \text{V}$. Is the reaction spontaneous?
4. How does $\Delta G^\circ$ relate to the equilibrium constant?
5. A student increases reactant concentration in an electrochemical cell. How does cell potential change (Nernst qualitative)?
6. In an electrolytic cell, which electrode do metal cations migrate toward?
7. A reaction has $\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K$. If $\Delta G^\circ = 0$, what is $K$?
8. How does temperature affect whether a reaction with $\Delta H > 0$ and $\Delta S > 0$ is thermodynamically favored?
9. In electrolysis of molten NaCl, what product forms at the cathode?
10. Which half-reaction has the more positive standard reduction potential, indicating it is more likely to be reduced?
11. ATP hydrolysis drives an unfavorable biosynthesis reaction. What type of thermodynamic coupling is this?
12. A galvanic cell approaches equilibrium. What happens to cell voltage?
13. Which spontaneous process illustrates the second law of thermodynamics through entropy increase?
14. Using Faraday's law, how many moles of Cu are deposited when 2 mol of electrons pass through a $Cu^{2+}$ solution?

### MCQ Plan (55 questions)
- **Easy (11):** Entropy sign prediction, ΔG sign from ΔH/ΔS, galvanic vs. electrolytic cell distinction
- **Medium (25):** ΔG calculations, cell potential from reduction potentials, Faraday's law calculations
- **Hard (19):** Temperature-dependent ΔG, Nernst equation qualitative reasoning, coupled reactions analysis, complex electrolysis problems
