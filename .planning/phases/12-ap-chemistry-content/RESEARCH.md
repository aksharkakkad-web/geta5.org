## Formula/Equation Reference Sheet (from CED Appendix 2)

All equations below are provided to students on the AP Chemistry Exam. Students are NOT required to memorize them. Therefore `name_to_formula` drills are prohibited.

### Atomic Structure
- $F_{\text{coulombic}} \propto \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$ (Coulomb's law)
- $E = h\nu$ (Planck's equation for photon energy)
- $c = \lambda\nu$ (wave equation)
- $n = \frac{m}{M}$ (moles = mass / molar mass)
- $N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}\ \text{mol}^{-1}$ (Avogadro's number)
- $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ (Planck's constant)
- $c = 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m·s}^{-1}$ (speed of light)

### Gases, Liquids, and Solutions
- $PV = nRT$ (Ideal gas law)
- $\frac{P_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 V_2}{T_2}$ (Combined gas law)
- $P_A = P_{\text{total}} \times X_A$, where $X_A = \frac{\text{moles A}}{\text{total moles}}$ (Dalton's partial pressure)
- $P_{\text{total}} = P_A + P_B + P_C + \ldots$
- $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ (kinetic energy)
- $M = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{L_{\text{solution}}}$ (molarity)
- $D = \frac{m}{V}$ (density)
- $A = \varepsilon bc$ (Beer-Lambert law)
- $R = 8.314\ \text{J·mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} = 0.08206\ \text{L·atm·mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$
- STP = 273.15 K and 1.0 atm; ideal gas at STP = 22.4 L/mol

### Kinetics
- Zeroth order: $[A]_t - [A]_0 = -kt$
- First order: $\ln[A]_t - \ln[A]_0 = -kt$
- Second order: $\frac{1}{[A]_t} - \frac{1}{[A]_0} = kt$
- Half-life (first order): $t_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{k}$

### Equilibrium
- $K_c = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}$ for $aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD$
- $K_p = \frac{(P_C)^c(P_D)^d}{(P_A)^a(P_B)^b}$
- $K_w = [\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{OH}^-] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$ at 25°C
- $\text{pH} = -\log[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+]$; $\text{pOH} = -\log[\text{OH}^-]$
- $\text{pK}_w = 14 = \text{pH} + \text{pOH}$ at 25°C
- $K_a = \frac{[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}$; $\text{pK}_a = -\log K_a$
- $K_b = \frac{[\text{OH}^-][\text{HB}^+]}{[\text{B}]}$; $\text{pK}_b = -\log K_b$
- $K_w = K_a \times K_b$; $\text{pK}_w = \text{pK}_a + \text{pK}_b$
- $\text{pH} = \text{pK}_a + \log\frac{[\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}$ (Henderson-Hasselbalch)

### Thermodynamics/Electrochemistry
- $q = mc\Delta T$ (heat transfer)
- $\Delta H^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \sum \Delta H^\circ_f(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta H^\circ_f(\text{reactants})$ (Hess's Law / formation enthalpies)
- $\Delta S^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \sum S^\circ(\text{products}) - \sum S^\circ(\text{reactants})$
- $\Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T\Delta S^\circ$ (Gibbs free energy)
- $\Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \sum \Delta G^\circ_f(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta G^\circ_f(\text{reactants})$
- $\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K$; $K = e^{-\Delta G^\circ/RT}$
- $\Delta G^\circ = -nFE^\circ$ (cell potential and free energy)
- $E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - \frac{RT}{nF}\ln Q$ (Nernst equation)
- $I = \frac{q}{t}$ (current = charge/time; Faraday's law)
- $F = 96,485\ \text{C·mol}^{-1}\text{e}^-$ (Faraday's constant)

## Per-Unit Research

### Unit 1: Atomic Structure and Properties (7–9%)
**Topics:** 1.1 Moles/Molar Mass, 1.2 Mass Spectra, 1.3 Elemental Composition, 1.4 Composition of Mixtures, 1.5 Atomic Structure/Electron Configuration, 1.6 Photoelectron Spectroscopy, 1.7 Periodic Trends, 1.8 Valence Electrons/Ionic Compounds

**Learning Objectives:**
- 1.1.A: Calculate moles using dimensional analysis and mole concept; EQN: $n = m/M$
- 1.2.A: Explain mass spectra relationship to isotope masses and abundance; average atomic mass from weighted average
- 1.3.A: Explain relationship between elemental composition by mass and empirical formula
- 1.4.A: Explain quantitative relationship between elemental composition and mixture composition
- 1.5.A: Represent ground-state electron configuration using Aufbau principle; Coulomb's law $F \propto \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$; shells/subshells; core vs. valence electrons
- 1.6.A: Explain relationship between PES spectrum and electron configuration / electron-nucleus interactions
- 1.7.A: Explain atomic property trends (ionization energy, atomic/ionic radii, electron affinity, electronegativity) using Coulomb's law, shell model, shielding, effective nuclear charge
- 1.8.A: Explain relationship between valence electrons and ionic compound formation

**Exclusions:** quantum numbers for subshell electrons NOT assessed; exceptions to Aufbau NOT assessed

**Key Terms (is_key_term candidates):** mole, molar mass, Avogadro's number, isotope, empirical formula, electron configuration, core electrons, valence electrons, ionization energy, electronegativity, atomic radius, photoelectron spectroscopy, effective nuclear charge, Aufbau principle, periodic trend

**Drill mode breakdown:**
- definition_to_term: mole, molar mass, isotope, empirical formula, electron configuration, core electrons, valence electrons, effective nuclear charge, shielding, Aufbau principle
- significance_to_person: Avogadro (Avogadro's number), Rutherford (nuclear model/gold foil), Bohr (energy levels), Coulomb (Coulomb's law)
- concept_mc: periodic trends application, PES interpretation, ionic charge prediction

- 3.3.A: Represent solid/liquid/gas phases with particulate model
- 3.4.A: Apply ideal gas law: $PV = nRT$; Dalton's law partial pressures
- 3.5.A: Explain KMT; Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution; $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$; temperature ∝ average KE

**Topics:** 5.1 Reaction Rates, 5.2 Introduction to Rate Law, 5.3 Concentration Changes Over Time, 5.4 Elementary Reactions, 5.5 Collision Model, 5.6 Reaction Energy Profile, 5.7 Introduction to Reaction Mechanisms, 5.8 Reaction Mechanism and Rate Law, 5.9 Pre-Equilibrium Approximation, 5.10 Multistep Reaction Energy Profile, 5.11 Catalysis

**Learning Objectives:**
- 5.1.A: Explain how concentration, temperature, surface area, catalysts affect rate

- 5.7.A: Reaction mechanism components: elementary steps, intermediates, catalysts
- 5.8.A: Rate law from mechanism with rate-limiting step
- 5.9.A: Pre-equilibrium approximation for non-rate-limiting first step
- 5.10.A: Multistep reaction energy profile
- 5.11.A: Catalysis: lowers activation energy, regenerated after reaction, surface catalysis, enzyme catalysis

### Unit 6: Thermochemistry (7–9%)
**Topics:** 6.1 Endothermic/Exothermic, 6.2 Energy Diagrams, 6.3 Heat Transfer/Thermal Equilibrium, 6.4 Heat Capacity/Calorimetry, 6.5 Energy of Phase Changes, 6.6 Introduction to Enthalpy, 6.7 Bond Enthalpies, 6.8 Enthalpy of Formation, 6.9 Hess's Law

**Learning Objectives:**
- 6.1.A: Explain endo/exothermic processes; system energy increases (endo) or decreases (exo)
- 6.2.A: Represent transformations with energy diagrams
- 6.3.A: Explain heat transfer via molecular collisions; thermal equilibrium
- 6.4.A: Calculate heat: $q = mc\Delta T$; first law of thermodynamics; calorimetry
- 6.5.A: Energy of phase changes; constant temperature during phase change; molar enthalpy of vaporization/fusion
- 6.6.A: Enthalpy change at constant pressure; exothermic: $\Delta H < 0$; endothermic: $\Delta H > 0$
- 6.7.A: Calculate $\Delta H$ from bond energies: $\Delta H = \sum(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum(\text{bonds formed})$
- 6.8.A: $\Delta H^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \sum\Delta H^\circ_f(\text{products}) - \sum\Delta H^\circ_f(\text{reactants})$
- 6.9.A/B: Hess's Law: reversing a reaction changes sign of $\Delta H$; multiplying changes $\Delta H$ proportionally; summing reactions sums $\Delta H$

**Exclusions:** technical distinctions between enthalpy and internal energy NOT assessed; state functions concept NOT assessed

**Key Terms:** endothermic, exothermic, enthalpy, calorimetry, specific heat capacity, heat of fusion, heat of vaporization, Hess's Law, standard enthalpy of formation, bond enthalpy

### Unit 7: Equilibrium (7–9%)
**Topics:** 7.1 Introduction to Equilibrium, 7.2 Direction of Reversible Reactions, 7.3 Reaction Quotient/Equilibrium Constant, 7.4 Calculating Equilibrium Constant, 7.5 Magnitude of Equilibrium Constant, 7.6 Properties of Equilibrium Constant, 7.7 Calculating Equilibrium Concentrations, 7.8 Representations of Equilibrium, 7.9 Le Châtelier's Principle, 7.10 Q and Le Châtelier's, 7.11 Solubility Equilibria, 7.12 Common-Ion Effect

**Learning Objectives:**
- 7.1.A: Explain equilibrium as dynamic state (equal forward/reverse rates); constant concentrations
- 7.2.A: Forward rate > reverse → net conversion to products; reverse > forward → net conversion to reactants
- 7.3.A: Write $K_c$ and $K_p$ expressions; $Q$ vs. $K$; solids and pure liquids omitted from expression
- 7.4.A: Calculate $K_c$ or $K_p$ from experimental data
- 7.5.A: Large $K$ → products favored; small $K$ → reactants favored
- 7.6.A: Reversal inverts $K$; multiplication raises $K$ to power; addition multiplies $K$ values
- 7.7.A: ICE table approach; $Q < K$ → forward; $Q > K$ → reverse; $Q = K$ → equilibrium
- 7.8.A: Particulate representations of equilibrium
- 7.9.A: Le Châtelier's principle: predict response to stress (concentration change, temperature change, volume/pressure change for gases, dilution)
- 7.10.A: Concentration stresses change $Q$ only; temperature changes $K$; system restores $Q = K$
- 7.11.A: Solubility product constant $K_{sp}$; calculate molar solubility from $K_{sp}$
- 7.12.A: Common-ion effect reduces solubility; qualitative Le Châtelier or $K_{sp}$ calculation

**Exclusions:** Kc ↔ Kp conversion NOT assessed; dissolved species in equilibrium with gas phase NOT assessed; polyprotic acid computations for titration curves NOT fully assessed

**Key Terms:** equilibrium constant, reaction quotient, Le Châtelier's principle, ICE table, solubility product constant, common-ion effect, dynamic equilibrium, $K_c$, $K_p$, $K_{sp}$

### Unit 8: Acids and Bases (11–15%)
**Topics:** 8.1 Introduction to Acids/Bases, 8.2 pH/pOH of Strong Acids/Bases, 8.3 Weak Acid/Base Equilibria, 8.4 Acid-Base Reactions/Buffers, 8.5 Acid-Base Titrations, 8.6 Molecular Structure of Acids/Bases, 8.7 pH and pKa, 8.8 Properties of Buffers, 8.9 Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation, 8.10 Buffer Capacity, 8.11 pH and Solubility

**Learning Objectives:**
- 8.1.A: $\text{pH} = -\log[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+]$; $\text{pOH} = -\log[\text{OH}^-]$; $K_w = [\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{OH}^-] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$ at 25°C; neutral: pH = pOH = 7.0 at 25°C
- 8.2.A: Strong acids (HCl, HBr, HI, HClO₄, H₂SO₄, HNO₃) fully dissociate; strong bases (group I/II hydroxides) fully dissociate
- 8.3.A: $K_a$ and $K_b$ expressions; percent ionization; $K_w = K_a \times K_b$
- 8.4.A: Strong acid + strong base: $H^+(aq) + OH^-(aq) \rightarrow H_2O(l)$; weak acid + strong base produces buffer or conjugate base; ICE calculations
- 8.5.A: Titration curves; equivalence point; half-equivalence point (pH = pKa); polyprotic acid titrations (qualitative)
- 8.6.A: Molecular structure determines acid strength; electronegative atoms stabilize conjugate base; carboxylic acids = weak acids; ammonia/carboxylate = weak bases
- 8.7.A: pH vs. pKa determines protonation state; acid-base indicators change color at their pKa
- 8.8.A: Buffer = large concentrations of conjugate acid-base pair; conjugate acid reacts with added base, conjugate base reacts with added acid
- 8.9.A: Henderson-Hasselbalch: $\text{pH} = \text{pK}_a + \log\frac{[\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}$
- 8.10.A: Buffer capacity increases with higher component concentrations; ratio determines pH preference
- 8.11.A: pH-sensitive solubility when constituent ion is weak acid/base or hydroxide (qualitative Le Châtelier)

**Exclusions:** Lewis acids/bases NOT assessed; computation of solubility as a function of pH NOT assessed; change in pH from acid/base addition to buffer NOT assessed; Henderson-Hasselbalch derivation NOT assessed

**Key Terms:** pH, pOH, $K_w$, strong acid, weak acid, $K_a$, $K_b$, pKa, buffer, Henderson-Hasselbalch, equivalence point, half-equivalence point, percent ionization, acid-base indicator, buffer capacity, conjugate acid-base pair

### Unit 9: Thermodynamics and Electrochemistry (7–9%)
**Topics:** 9.1 Introduction to Entropy, 9.2 Absolute Entropy/Entropy Change, 9.3 Gibbs Free Energy/Thermodynamic Favorability, 9.4 Thermodynamic and Kinetic Control, 9.5 Free Energy and Equilibrium, 9.6 Free Energy of Dissolution, 9.7 Coupled Reactions, 9.8 Galvanic/Electrolytic Cells, 9.9 Cell Potential/Free Energy, 9.10 Cell Potential Under Nonstandard Conditions, 9.11 Electrolysis/Faraday's Law

**Learning Objectives:**
- 9.1.A: Identify sign/magnitude of entropy change; solid→liquid→gas increases entropy; more moles of gas products → higher entropy; higher temperature → higher entropy
- 9.2.A: $\Delta S^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \sum S^\circ(\text{products}) - \sum S^\circ(\text{reactants})$
- 9.3.A: $\Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T\Delta S^\circ$; thermodynamically favored when $\Delta G^\circ < 0$; $\Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \sum\Delta G^\circ_f(\text{products}) - \sum\Delta G^\circ_f(\text{reactants})$; predict from signs of $\Delta H$ and $\Delta S$
- 9.4.A: Thermodynamically favored but kinetically controlled reactions (high activation energy)
- 9.5.A: $\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K$; $K = e^{-\Delta G^\circ/RT}$; $\Delta G^\circ < 0$ → $K > 1$; $\Delta G^\circ > 0$ → $K < 1$
- 9.6.A: Free energy of dissolution depends on breaking lattice forces, solvent reorganization, solute-solvent interactions
- 9.7.A: Coupled reactions: thermodynamically unfavorable reaction driven by favorable one (e.g., ATP→ADP in biology); $\Delta G^\circ_{\text{overall}} < 0$
- 9.8.A: Galvanic cell (thermodynamically favored; positive voltage); electrolytic cell (thermodynamically unfavored; requires external energy); oxidation at anode; reduction at cathode; salt bridge maintains electrical neutrality
- 9.9.A: $\Delta G^\circ = -nFE^\circ$; positive $E^\circ$ → thermodynamically favored; standard cell potential from standard reduction potentials
- 9.10.A: Nernst equation: $E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - \frac{RT}{nF}\ln Q$ (qualitative understanding); cell potential decreases toward equilibrium ($E = 0$ at equilibrium); Nernst qualitative only — algorithmic calculations NOT primary assessment
- 9.11.A: Faraday's laws; $I = q/t$; $F = 96,485\ \text{C·mol}^{-1}$; calculate moles of electrons, mass deposited/removed, current, time

**Exclusions:** Nernst equation algorithmic calculation is insufficient alone — conceptual reasoning required; labeling electrodes as positive/negative NOT assessed

**Key Terms:** entropy, Gibbs free energy, thermodynamically favored, kinetic control, galvanic cell, electrolytic cell, anode, cathode, standard reduction potential, Faraday's constant, electrolysis, coupled reactions
